I don't build side projects to have something on a resume. I build them because there are problems I want to understand deeply, and the only way to understand a system is to build one.

This post is an honest account of what I'm building in 2026, what I'm trying to prove with each project, and why I chose these problems over the thousand other problems I could have picked.

## The four projects

**Sentinel** is an AI code review assistant — but the interesting part isn't the AI. It's the eval harness. I hand-labeled 100 real pull requests across four categories (security, logic, style, performance) to create a ground-truth dataset. Every model run is scored against that dataset with precision, recall, and F1 per category. The thesis: if you can't measure your AI's output quality, you don't have a product — you have a demo.

**Kairos** is a multi-tenant OKR platform, and the interesting part isn't the product. It's the data isolation model. The entire multi-tenancy strategy is built on Postgres Row Level Security policies rather than application-layer WHERE clauses. Every RLS policy gets tested: can tenant A see tenant B's objectives? The thesis: security belongs at the database layer, not the application layer. If your app crashes, your data should still be safe.

**Helios** is a distributed time-series database written from scratch in Go. LSM-tree storage engine with a write-ahead log, Gorilla encoding for compression, Raft consensus for replication across three nodes, and a PromQL parser and evaluator on top. No InfluxDB, no Prometheus internals borrowed. The thesis: you don't understand a system until you've built one. Every decision in this codebase forces you to confront trade-offs that library users never see.

**NeuroLens** is adversarial robustness research for multimodal AI systems. The attack surface I'm exploring: what happens when you apply adversarial perturbations to images fed into CLIP-style models? The perturbation is imperceptible to humans but corrupts the vision-language alignment in ways that downstream models can't detect. This became an arXiv preprint. The thesis: multimodal models have attack surfaces that unimodal robustness research doesn't cover, and the field needs benchmarks.

## Why these four?

Three reasons. First, they span the stack in a way that matters — systems (Helios), product (Kairos), AI evaluation (Sentinel), and research (NeuroLens). If you want to work at the intersection of production systems and applied AI, you need fluency in all of it.

Second, each one has a hard problem at its center that I don't already know how to solve. Sentinel's hardest problem is annotation quality and bias in the ground-truth dataset. Kairos's hardest problem is writing RLS policies that are both correct and performant at scale. Helios's hardest problem is Raft leader election under network partition. NeuroLens's hardest problem is defining a meaningful metric for cross-modal semantic corruption.

Third, they're all unfinished in interesting ways. I could polish Kairos's UI for another six months and call it done. But the thing I actually want to ship is a provably correct RLS implementation with a test suite that verifies isolation properties. That's harder to ship, and harder to explain, and more worth building.

## What I want these to prove

I want to demonstrate three things that most portfolios don't show: that I can build systems that measure their own quality, that I understand trade-offs at the infrastructure layer and not just the application layer, and that I can take a research problem from idea to preprint.

These aren't projects I'm building to get a job. They're projects I'm building because they're interesting, and I happen to think the kind of engineer who builds interesting things tends to be the kind of engineer that companies building at scale want to hire.
